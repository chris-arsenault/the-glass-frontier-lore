# Hab Meridian

The ring hab that first reconnected with [Glasswake](glasswake.md), and which has, in the generations since, transformed that founding moment into the entirety of its civic identity. Hab Meridian today is a living-history hab — a working museum operating at hab scale, where one full segment of the ring's habitable arc is preserved in early-Rekindling period, staffed by residents who have made the reconnection era their life's work, and visited by anyone in the system curious about what those years actually felt like.

The hab has banked on the reconnection. There is no other Meridian. There is no contemporary Meridian. The hab does one thing and does it as completely as a community can do anything.

## The First Signal <!-- Origin -->

The reconnection happened in 2305 CE. A salvage crew in [Glasswake](glasswake.md) repaired a half-buried pre-Glassfall transmitter and pushed a signal into orbit; Hab Meridian's standing watch — a single technician on a long shift, working an old receiver that nobody had expected to ever do anything — heard the carrier wave come in, recognized it for what it was, and answered. The first cross-channel conversation in sixty years lasted forty-three minutes and was largely about whether the signal was real.

The conversation was recorded. The Hab Meridian side of the recording survives in the hab's archives. The Glasswake side does not survive — the transmitter shorted out three days later and the recording medium degraded — but the Meridian recording captures both sides of the exchange. The technician who answered the call, **Aless Vorrith**, lived to be ninety-one years old and spent the last forty years of her life giving the same five-minute talk to visiting groups, three times a day, in the small chamber where she had taken the signal. She was buried in the hab. Her successor in the role is the great-granddaughter of her apprentice and gives a version of the same five-minute talk.

The hab's continuing relationship with Glasswake is one of the closest cross-orbit partnerships in the system. Trade flows freely between them. Delegations cross at the major civic anniversaries. The two communities' children study each other's histories as a matter of course. But the relationship is a working one, not a sentimental one. The sentimental work happens elsewhere in the hab.

## The Period Arc <!-- Atmosphere -->

A ring hab is a torus, and Hab Meridian's habitable interior is divided into two roughly equal arcs by old structural seams. The Period Arc is one of them.

The Period Arc is the museum.

The arc has been deliberately preserved in 2305-era condition — walls, lighting, signage, infrastructure, even the standing background hum of the resonance environment, all maintained to match what the hab looked and sounded like during the founding decades of the Rekindling. The arc's residents — about four thousand of them, roughly a fifth of the hab's total population — live in period dress, use period equipment to the extent that period equipment can be safely maintained, and conduct daily life in a working approximation of the reconnection era. They are not actors. They are residents. They were born in the arc, or moved to it as adults, and they have made the work of being Hab Meridian's living memory their actual life.

Visitors enter the arc through a single transition gate at the segment boundary. Inside, the arc operates on its own civic schedule: period markets, period workshops, period schools, period civic council. The radio room where Aless Vorrith took the first call is preserved at the heart of the arc and is staffed continuously by descendants and apprentices of the original watch. The streets are walked. The conversations are had. The work — small-scale agriculture, period craft production, basic mechanical maintenance using carefully reconstructed tools — is real work that produces real goods. The arc imports modern medical care and a few other practical necessities through the boundary gate, and the residents are aware that the necessities exist. They simply do not use them inside the arc.

The Period Arc receives several thousand visitors a month at peak. School groups from across the system. Researchers from the [Echo Ledger Conclave](../../npcs/factions/echo-ledger-conclave.md) (who consider the arc a useful primary source and an unsettling one). Diplomats and dignitaries on official courtesy visits. Tourists. The Hab Meridian visitor service handles arrivals with a practiced courtesy that is itself slightly period.

## The Other Arc <!-- Mechanics -->

The hab's other arc — informally called the **Outer**, by the period residents, or simply **the rest** — is not preserved. It is a working ring hab segment with modern infrastructure, modern resonance equipment, and a population of about fifteen thousand who run the hab.

The Outer is, by any honest accounting, an institutional support structure for the Period Arc. The hab council that governs Meridian sits in the Outer. The hab's medical and educational infrastructure sits in the Outer. The visitor logistics — arrivals, departures, dormitories for school groups, the diplomatic guest quarters — sit in the Outer. The Outer trades with the rest of the system for the goods the hab does not produce internally, manages the hab's relationships with [Vantara](../../npcs/factions/vantara.md) and the [Tempered Accord](../../npcs/factions/tempered-accord.md), and runs the modern hab life that the Period Arc has deliberately stepped out of.

The Outer's residents are not actors either. They are administrators, technicians, teachers, doctors, dock workers. Most of them grew up in the hab and chose the Outer rather than the Period Arc as adults. Some of them rotate into the Period Arc for years and back. The relationship between the two arcs is functional, professional, and not entirely affectionate. Period residents consider the Outer necessary infrastructure. Outer residents consider the Period Arc the reason the hab exists. Both views are correct.

The hab has no other industries. There is no manufacturing for export. There is no significant resonance work. There is no scholarship that is not, in some way, about the hab's own history. The hab is a museum, plus the institutional apparatus required to run a museum at hab scale, plus the demographic footprint that supports both.

## How It Pays For Itself <!-- Economy -->

The hab's economy runs on five overlapping flows.

**Visitor revenue.** Admission to the Period Arc, dormitory fees for visiting groups, services for long-stay scholars, the small-trade economy that surrounds any tourist destination. This is the largest single flow and the most variable.

**Accord and Sithari subsidies.** Hab Meridian is one of the few standing institutional partnerships the [Tempered Accord](../../npcs/factions/tempered-accord.md) directly funds — the Accord considers the hab's preservation work a civic asset of system-wide value. Sithari supplements through a long-standing diplomatic appropriation that nobody has tried to revoke and that no Sithari political faction has political reasons to revoke. The subsidies are not enormous but they are steady.

**Conclave service contracts.** The [Echo Ledger Conclave](../../npcs/factions/echo-ledger-conclave.md) maintains a permanent research presence in the hab, paying for access to the archived founding-era records and for the unique ability to study the period as a working environment. The contract is small and prestigious and the relationship between the two institutions is professionally cordial.

**Glasswake partnership trade.** The Plymouth Rock partnership produces real trade, primarily in goods that the hab cannot produce internally and that Glasswake can supply. The exchange runs in both directions; Meridian produces some period-craft goods that have a small but reliable market in surface settlements that appreciate the provenance.

**Endowment.** The hab has accumulated, across more than a century of operation, an endowment fund that absorbs revenue volatility. The endowment is conservatively managed. The hab council has a standing policy of never spending capital. Whether the policy will survive a sufficiently bad year remains untested.

## The Critique <!-- Tensions -->

Not everyone in the system finds Hab Meridian's choice admirable. The standard critique, voiced more politely in some quarters than in others, is that the hab has surrendered the possibility of becoming anything else. The Period Arc is impressive; the question is what Meridian could have been if it had not committed so completely to being what it was. The hab has no contemporary culture of its own. It has no contemporary art, no contemporary science, no contemporary anything that is not in some way commentary on or service to the founding era. A century and a third of civic life and the hab's residents have made nothing that is theirs in the way the [Fermata Station](fermata-station.md) jazz governance, or the [Sithari](sithari.md) ringglass aesthetic, or the [Pelhari](pelhari.md) instrument-wright tradition is theirs.

The Meridian council has heard the critique often enough to have prepared responses. The standard response is that the hab is doing something nobody else has the institutional commitment to do, that the work is genuinely valuable to a system that would otherwise lose its founding memory, and that the choice to specialize in this way was made openly and democratically across generations. The standard response is not entirely persuasive to everyone who hears it. It does not need to be. The hab continues.

Inside the hab, the critique exists too — most actively among Outer residents in middle age, who occasionally wonder out loud what they would have made of themselves elsewhere. The Period Arc residents are largely insulated from the question by the structure of their commitment. The hab's young people leave or stay in roughly the same proportions as other habs; those who stay sort themselves into the two arcs by temperament. The system continues.

## Present Day <!-- Present Day -->

Hab Meridian in 2438 CE is one of the most stable institutions in the system. The visitor traffic has been steady for decades. The Period Arc's preservation work is in good order. The hab council is competent. The relationships with Glasswake, Sithari, and the Accord are durable. The hab is not growing and not shrinking. It is doing what it has done for one hundred and thirty-three years.

There is a small ongoing argument, mostly internal, about whether the Period Arc should be expanded to preserve later founding-era developments — the 2310s, the 2320s, the early Contested Reach period. The argument has not been resolved. The hab's institutional disposition is conservative; the existing arc covers the founding decade, and the decade is what matters most, and expansion would dilute the focus. The argument will continue. The hab will continue.

The signal Aless Vorrith answered in 2305 is still played, daily, in the radio room she once worked in, by people whose grandparents knew her. The conversation is forty-three minutes long. Visitors who want to hear the whole thing have to schedule a slot. Most do.
