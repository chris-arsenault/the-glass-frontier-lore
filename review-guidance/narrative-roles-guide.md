# Narrative Roles: Viewpoints and Titans

NPCs in the lore fall into three tiers. Most are regular NPCs — named individuals with a few relationships and a role in the world. Two elevated tiers exist for characters that serve specific narrative functions: **viewpoints** and **titans**.

These tiers are not about power, prestige, or prominence. They describe how a character relates to the narrative and — critically — how a player would interact with them.

## Viewpoint Characters

A viewpoint character is an NPC whose story a player enters. The player arrives mid-arc — the viewpoint's problems predate the player and will outlast them. The player investigates, witnesses, assists, or complicates the viewpoint's story. The player does not own it.

Viewpoint characters are the primary generators of world detail. Their backstories create the need for minor entities — forgotten locations, marginal factions, personal incidents, specific artifacts. These minor entities exist because the viewpoint character interacts with them. This is how the world gets big without feeling artificially inflated: every minor entity exists because someone specific cares about it.

### Structural Properties

**Thread/theme anchor.** Each viewpoint character is tied to one or more narrative threads and embodies a thematic question. Dern Talish fills beats on the Bloom Containment thread and embodies "what does holding the line cost?" Lira Vashtenri embodies "what happens when instinct outperforms theory?" The viewpoint is the player's way into that thread.

**Temporal depth.** Viewpoint characters need a "before" — who they were before the narrative found them. This is the investigative surface: the player discovers the viewpoint's backstory through play, not exposition. The entry must have enough history to support discovery.

**A current problem the player can't fully solve.** The viewpoint character has something unresolved that belongs to them. Dern's DM secret. Lira's supply chain negotiations. The player can affect outcomes but cannot own the resolution. Restriction of player power is the mechanism that gives the NPC agency.

**A cloud of minor entities.** Viewpoint characters generate shells. A specific refinery, an orphanage hab, a mentor, a patrol route, a personal incident. These entities are forgotten or marginal prominence — no one else in the world cares about them. They exist because the viewpoint character's story requires them.

**Specific, high-fidelity connections.** Each connection has a start date, a specific role, and a specific tension. Not generic memberships. The viewpoint connects to 2-3 major entities with depth, and to many minor entities that only they link to.

### Graph Signature

- **Degree:** High (10-20+), but connections skew toward forgotten/marginal entities
- **Unique connections:** Many. Entities that only this viewpoint links to.
- **Thread connections:** FILLS_BEAT on one or more threads
- **Temporal edges:** Rich temporal data. Role changes, employment history, relocations.
- **Prominence:** Typically recognized or marginal. Narrative centrality and public awareness are independent.
- **Shell generation:** Writing a viewpoint entry should produce 5-10 new shell entities.

### Prose Signature

- Detail about daily work, habits, specific tools and places
- At least one section about who they were before the interesting thing happened
- Personal stakes distinct from civilizational stakes
- Friction with their own role, faction, or circumstances
- Other characters' opinions about them (but not universally — they're not famous)

### Detection (is this NPC a viewpoint?)

1. **Removal test:** Remove them. Does a narrative thread lose its ground-level anchor?
2. **Story test:** Can you pitch a short story about them in one sentence that isn't "they're important"?
3. **Perspective test:** Would telling a key event through their eyes reveal something no other character's eyes would?
4. **Generation test:** Does fleshing out their backstory create the need for new entries?
5. **Player test:** Could a player spend 2-3 sessions interacting with this character's story?

### Writing Rules

1. **Detail their work, not their status.** "She maintains ringglass arrays by feel" is viewpoint-grade. "She is a renowned engineer" is not.
2. **Show the before.** At least one section on who they were before the narrative found them.
3. **Generate minor entities.** Their backstory should create shells. If writing a viewpoint's entry doesn't produce new entries, the backstory isn't specific enough.
4. **Don't route the world through them.** They witness and participate in events. They don't cause all of them. Thematic convergence, not character convergence.
5. **Let prominence be low.** A viewpoint can be marginal. The player discovers them; the world doesn't celebrate them.

## Titan Characters

A titan is an NPC (or institution) whose decisions shaped the world the player walks through. The player navigates conditions the titan created. Titans are encountered through reputation first, then brief appearances, then eventual engagement.

Titans are not quest givers. They are the reason quests exist. The Bloom Zones exist because something destabilized the resonance ecology. The Tempered Accord exists because someone brokered peace. The player interacts with consequences of titans long before interacting with titans directly.

The literary precedent: Anomander Rake never gets a POV chapter. Tywin Lannister never gets a POV chapter. The most powerful characters in these worlds are seen from below, through witnesses. Mystique comes from restraint.

The game precedent: Arthas appears briefly across an entire expansion. The player builds toward confrontation across hours of play. Arasaka in Cyberpunk is the reason Night City is the way it is — the player fights the consequences long before fighting the corporation.

### Structural Properties

**Known through impact.** Other entries reference the titan. NPCs have opinions about them. The player builds a picture from fragments across multiple entries before encountering the titan's own entry.

**Actions that created present-day conditions.** The titan's entry focuses on what they did that is still live — things the player will navigate. Not a biography; a record of consequences.

**Sparse direct characterization.** The titan's own entry is shorter than a viewpoint's. Internal experience is withheld or minimal. What they think is less important than what they did.

**Generates renowned shells.** Titans don't only connect to existing entities — deciding on a titan now will generate shell entities at the renowned/mythic level that need filling in later. A titan's faction, their signature act, their seat of power — these are the large-scale entities the world needs.

### Graph Signature

- **Degree:** Moderate (5-10). Fewer connections than viewpoints, but to heavier entities.
- **Connection prominence skew:** Mostly renowned/mythic entities
- **Thread connections:** Climactic beats or none. Titans don't fill incremental thread beats.
- **Prominence:** Renowned or mythic
- **Shell generation:** Produces renowned/mythic shells — major factions, major incidents, major locations.

### Prose Signature

- Described through observed impact, not internal experience
- Other characters have opinions about them (and disagree)
- Shorter entries — mystique comes from restraint
- Actions have system-wide consequences
- No daily-life detail. Titans operate at altitude.

### Detection (is this NPC a titan?)

1. **Condition test:** Did this entity's actions create conditions that other entities now navigate?
2. **Reputation test:** Do multiple unrelated entries reference them?
3. **Altitude test:** Is their perspective too high to be useful as a ground-level viewpoint?
4. **Scale test:** Do their consequences span the system, not just a region or faction?

### Writing Rules

1. **Show impact, not interiority.** Describe what they did and what it caused. Withhold what they thought.
2. **Let other entries do the characterization.** The titan's entry is their record. Other entries contain the opinions, the fear, the reverence.
3. **Keep it short.** If a titan's entry is longer than a viewpoint's, something is wrong.
4. **Don't explain their motivations fully.** Ambiguity is a feature. The player should wonder.

## The Relationship Between Viewpoints and Titans

Viewpoints exist in the shadow of titans. A viewpoint might work for a titan's faction, live in a place a titan controls, or deal with consequences of a titan's decisions — but the connection is often indirect. The viewpoint's entry may name the titan; the titan's entry rarely names the viewpoint.

The narrative engine traverses both:
- **Thread → viewpoint → minor entities** for ground-level story detail
- **Thread → titan → major entities** for civilizational-scale context

Both paths can serve the same thread from different altitudes. The player experiences the viewpoint path first (personal, investigative, ground-level) and the titan path later (confrontational, revelatory, system-scale).

### Asymmetric Awareness

| | Viewpoint knows about titan? | Titan knows about viewpoint? |
|---|---|---|
| Default | Yes — they navigate the titan's consequences | No — the viewpoint is below their notice |
| Exceptional | — | When the titan's story intersects the viewpoint's (dramatic peak) |

When a titan becomes aware of a viewpoint character, that is a narrative event — not the default state.

## Applying the Frontmatter

Viewpoint and titan are not new entity types — they are still `type: npc`. They are marked with a **`narrative_role`** frontmatter field:

```yaml
---
title: Dern Talish
type: npc
narrative_role: viewpoint
tags: [resonance, containment]
prominence: recognized
---
```

```yaml
---
title: The Adversary
type: dm
narrative_role: titan
tags: [corruption, resonance]
prominence: mythic
dm: true
---
```

The `narrative_role` field is optional. Most NPCs don't have one. When present, it signals elevated treatment per this guide.
