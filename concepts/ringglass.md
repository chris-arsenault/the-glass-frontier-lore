# Ringglass

Ringglass is the crystalline material the orbital rings were built from. It is the physical basis for all [resonance](../cosmology/resonance.md) technology. Without it, nothing works.

## Sources

Ringglass enters the economy from three places:

**Fallout deposits.** 130 years of debris settling from the [Glass Frontier](../cosmology/the-glass-frontier.md) has seeded Kaleidos's surface with ringglass. It's in the soil, the riverbeds, the rock strata. Surface mining operations extract it from concentrated deposits — places where geography funneled the fallout into accumulation zones. This is the most common source and produces the lowest-grade material: small fragments, mixed purity, inconsistent resonance alignment. Good enough for construction aggregate and basic household tools. Not much else.

**Verge salvage.** The debris field between Kaleidos's atmosphere and the ring habs — the Verge — is dense with ringglass fragments, including pieces large enough to be commercially valuable. Salvage crews fly into the Verge, harvest fragments, and bring them down. This is dangerous, competitive, and lucrative. Verge-sourced ringglass is higher grade than surface deposits — larger pieces, better crystal structure, more consistent alignment. The best salvage comes from intact structural sections of the original ring, which can yield ringglass that's already tuned to specific frequencies and just needs cutting.

**Hab reclamation.** Some ring habs have more ringglass than they need — either from their own structure or from debris that's accumulated against their hulls over the decades. These habs sell surplus material, usually at premium prices, because hab-sourced ringglass tends to be the highest quality available: large, pure, and often still carrying its original tuning from the ring era.

## Grades

Not all ringglass is equal. The trade recognizes three broad grades:

**Aggregate.** Small fragments, mixed crystal structure, weak or inconsistent resonance. Cheap and abundant. Used in construction (ringglass-laced concrete, reinforced foundations), basic tools (lanterns, stoves, simple heaters), and as feedstock for refineries that process it into higher grades. Most of what's mined from surface deposits is aggregate.

**Refined.** Processed ringglass that's been cut, purified, and tuned to a specific frequency band. This is the standard commercial product — the material that powers kite-sails, runs communication arrays, and drives industrial equipment. Producing refined ringglass requires specialized cutting equipment and a skilled tuner who can align the crystal structure to the desired frequency without cracking it. Refineries exist on the surface, in the Verge, and in several ring habs. The process is well-understood but not simple; a bad cut ruins the piece.

**Intact.** Ringglass that retains its original ring-era configuration — large sections with complex, multi-frequency tuning that no modern refinery can replicate. Intact ringglass is rare, extremely valuable, and the subject of intense competition between factions, collectors, and governments. Most of it comes from Verge salvage or hab reclamation. Some pieces contain frequency configurations that nobody alive fully understands, hinting at capabilities the original builders had that haven't been recovered.

## Processing

Turning raw ringglass into something useful is a physical process, not a mystical one.

**Sorting.** Raw material is tested for resonance response — essentially, you vibrate it and see what comes back. This determines the crystal's natural frequency affinity and purity. Automated sorting equipment exists, but experienced sorters working by feel can be faster and more accurate for high-value pieces.

**Cutting.** Ringglass is cut along its crystal planes to isolate sections with consistent structure. Cut wrong, and the internal stresses fracture the piece, killing its resonance. Cut right, and you get a clean section that vibrates true. Cutting equipment ranges from industrial saws for aggregate processing to precision tools for refined work. The best cutters are artisans.

**Tuning.** The key step for refined ringglass. A tuned piece is one that's been adjusted — through controlled pressure, heat, and sympathetic vibration from already-tuned reference crystals — to resonate at a specific target frequency. The tuner aligns the crystal's internal structure so it preferentially vibrates at the desired frequency and dampens others. This is skilled work. A good tuner can feel when the crystal is approaching alignment. A great tuner can coax a marginal piece into a grade above its natural quality.

**Mounting.** Finished ringglass is mounted in housings appropriate to its use — metal frames for structural applications, insulated casings for kinetic arrays, shielded enclosures for signal equipment. The housing matters. Ringglass resonates through physical contact with surrounding materials, so what it's mounted in affects its performance.

## Trade

Ringglass is the most important commodity in the [Kaleidos system](../cosmology/the-kaleidos-system.md). Its trade defines economies, funds factions, and drives conflict.

**Aggregate** moves in bulk. It's heavy, cheap per unit, and consumed in enormous quantities by the construction industry. Bulk transport from mining regions to population centers is steady, boring, essential work.

**Refined** ringglass is where the money is. A single well-tuned kinetic array crystal is worth more than a ton of aggregate. Refined product moves along established trade routes, usually under escort, from refineries to buyers. The major factions all have stakes in the refining industry — either running their own operations or taxing those that pass through their territory.

**Intact** ringglass doesn't really have a market in the normal sense. Pieces surface irregularly from salvage operations and hab sales. When one appears, interested parties negotiate, bid, or occasionally fight over it. The value of a given piece depends entirely on what it does — and sometimes, nobody knows what it does until they've studied it for months.

## Scarcity

Ringglass is not running out — the debris field is vast and surface deposits are deep. But high-grade material is finite and getting harder to find. The easy Verge salvage was picked over decades ago. The richest surface deposits are claimed and defended. And intact ringglass, by definition, can't be manufactured; there's only what the original builders made.

This creates a slow squeeze. Aggregate stays cheap. Refined stays profitable but competitive. And intact pieces become rarer and more valuable with every year that passes.
