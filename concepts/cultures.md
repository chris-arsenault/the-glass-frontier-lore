# Cultures

Cultures in the Kaleidos system are orthogonal to species. A Silvarian orc and a Silvarian human share more cultural identity — language patterns, naming conventions, visual aesthetics, clothing — than a Silvarian human and a Hab-Worlder human. Species determines biology. Culture determines how you introduce yourself.

Cultures are not ideologies or governments. A single culture can contain many political factions, governance structures, and belief systems. What a culture provides is the surface layer: how people talk, how they dress, how they name their children, and how they recognize each other across a crowded station.

## Naming Conventions

Each culture defines distinct naming patterns. These are the primary species-agnostic cultural signal — hearing a name tells you where someone is from, regardless of what they are. Name generation follows patterns defined in `../the-canonry` name-forge for lexographic consistency.

## Initial Cultures

### Silvari

The culture of Kaleidos prime — specifically the settlements around the de facto capital region. Silvarian identity carries weight across the system the way a major Earth city's culture radiates outward. Cosmopolitan, multilingual, politically connected. Old-world prestige that sometimes curdles into old-world arrogance.

Silvari naming conventions, dress, and language patterns are the system's default "formal" register. Diplomats, Accord officials, and people who want to be taken seriously in inter-settlement politics tend to adopt Silvarian mannerisms even if they weren't born to them. This is resented by roughly everyone who isn't Silvarian, and exploited by roughly everyone who is.

Not a monoculture — Silvari contains enormous internal diversity in politics, belief, and lifestyle. What it provides is an aesthetic and linguistic common ground for the system's most connected population center.

### Hab-Worlder

The shared identity of people raised in ring habs. Not a single culture so much as a cultural family — Hab-Worlders from different habs can be as different from each other as any two surface settlements. What they share is the experience of growing up in sealed environments: an intuitive understanding of pressure, atmosphere, structural integrity, and the constant low-level awareness that the walls are all that stand between you and vacuum.

Hab-Worlder naming patterns are distinct from surface cultures but vary significantly by hab. The shared identity is more about mutual recognition — two Hab-Worlders meeting on a surface station will identify each other within minutes through body language, spatial habits, and the specific way they check exits when entering a room.

The cultural range is enormous. Fermata Station is Hab-Worlder. So is every other ring hab with its own micro-culture. The Hab-Worlder label is the broadest bucket — it says "I grew up in a can" and nothing more specific.
