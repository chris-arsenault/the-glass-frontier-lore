# Species

The Kaleidos system is home to multiple intelligent species. This is unremarkable to its inhabitants — multi-species coexistence predates living memory for everyone except possibly the [Absent](the-absent.md). The origin of this diversity is debated: accelerated divergent evolution driven by [resonance](../cosmology/resonance.md), deliberate bioengineering by the ring-era elves, independent alien arrival, or some combination. The honest answer is that nobody knows for certain, and most people don't think about it much.

All known species are roughly bipedal and person-shaped. None are aquatic or subterranean by nature, though individuals and communities adapt to various environments including ring habs, orbital stations, and off-world colonies.

## Defined Species

### Elves

See [The Absent](the-absent.md). Native to the Kaleidos system. Primary architects of the orbital ring. Physically similar to humans — taller, elongated ears, finer bone density, lifespans measured in centuries. Vanished during the Signal Famine. Their resonance science was centuries ahead of anyone else's; the gap they left is still felt.

### Humans

Alien arrivals, reaching the Kaleidos system around 2050 CE during the ring era. Integrated into the existing multi-species civilization. No special resonance affinity — humans can learn attunement through training, but it doesn't come naturally the way it does for elves or gnomes. Represent the largest single population in the present-day system, partly because their shorter lifespans mean faster generational turnover.

## Initial Species

### Orcs

Alien origin. Arrived in the system independently — not engineered, not native. The common fantasy stereotype is inverted here: orcs are *beautiful*. Tall, striking, physically powerful, with an aesthetic sensibility that other species find arresting. They are not dumb. The "strong and simple" reputation is a cultural misread by species who mistake a preference for directness as a lack of sophistication.

Their relationship with resonance, their cultural specifics, and their role in the present-day system need fleshing out.

### Gnomes

Bioengineered by the elves during the ring era. Their biology incorporates ringglass at a fundamental level — crystalline structures threaded through their bone and nervous system. This makes them naturally attuned to resonance in ways that other species have to train for years to approximate. A gnome can feel the resonance landscape the way others feel temperature.

This is useful and disturbing in roughly equal measure. Gnomes were *made* — designed to interface with the ring's systems. The ethical implications of that are a live issue in the present day, even though nobody alive was involved in the decision. Gnomes themselves have complicated feelings about it. Some consider their attunement a gift. Others consider it a brand.

The "part glass" aspect is literal — gnomish skin has a faint crystalline translucency in strong light, and their bones produce a barely audible harmonic hum at rest. Whether this is beautiful or unsettling depends on who you ask. Gnomes generally find the question tiresome.

### Fae

Engineered origin — created or modified during the ring era, though the specifics are debated. Their defining characteristic is spatial dislocation: fae have trouble maintaining a coherent physical presence. Their default state is *diffuse* — spread across a larger area than a single body should occupy, partially present in multiple locations, edges blurred.

In their diffuse state, fae think very slowly. Perception works, but cognition drops to a crawl — like being half-asleep across a wide area. To think clearly, act deliberately, or interact meaningfully with other species, a fae must *concentrate* their spatial identity into a coherent form. This takes effort and cannot be maintained indefinitely. Fae cycle between periods of coherent presence and diffuse rest.

This is not a power. It is a disability that fae have built an entire set of coping mechanisms around. Coherence aids — resonance-tuned personal devices, architectural accommodations, social structures that respect diffuse-time — are a significant part of fae material culture. The experience of being fae is, in part, the experience of constantly working to stay *here*.
