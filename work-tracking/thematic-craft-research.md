# Thematic Craft Research

Research on how top-tier science fiction and fantasy authors handle themes,
plot threads, and narrative structure. Intended to inform thematic anchoring
for The Glass Frontier without falling into amateur/generic framing.

---

## 1. How Professional Authors Frame Themes

### Isaac Asimov — Foundation

Asimov never names his central theme as an abstraction. Instead, he
**invents a fictional science** — psychohistory — and makes it the
mechanism through which the theme operates. The tension between individual
agency and historical determinism is not stated; it is *the plot engine*.

Key structural choices:

- **Psychohistory works on populations, not individuals.** This is stated
  as a scientific limitation, not a philosophical position. The theme
  emerges from the gap between what the science can do and what it cannot.
- **The Mule exists to test the thesis.** A mutant with unpredictable
  mental powers derails the Seldon Plan — the theme of individual vs.
  collective is dramatized by a character who *breaks the system*, not by
  characters who *discuss the system*.
- **The structure came from Gibbon.** Asimov modeled Foundation on Edward
  Gibbon's *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*,
  which he had read in its entirety twice. The galactic empire's collapse
  mirrors Rome's. The theme arrives through historical analogy, not
  philosophical declaration.

Asimov's own framing (1987, *Science Fiction Studies*): he described
psychohistory as "in a sense, the struggle between free will and
determinism." Note: he said *in a sense* — even describing his own work,
he framed the theme through the mechanism, not the abstraction.

On the relationship between knowledge and freedom: "Human freedom does
not exist in the absolute, but is conditioned by a host of determinisms.
In return, humans can influence and emancipate themselves from these
determinisms by becoming aware of how they affect them." The theme is
dialectical, not binary.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** The theme is not named. It is a *system*
that the reader encounters through plot. The system has rules, limits,
and failure modes, and those structural features ARE the thematic argument.

### Arthur C. Clarke — 2001, Childhood's End, Rendezvous with Rama

Clarke's recurring theme — humanity encountering intelligence so advanced
it is indistinguishable from divinity — is never named as a theme. It is
dramatized through *absence and scale*.

Key structural choices:

- **The aliens in 2001 are never depicted.** Stanley Kubrick and Clarke
  consulted Carl Sagan, who advised: don't depict them at all. The
  monolith is a *system artifact* — an object that implies an intelligence
  without representing it. The theme of human inadequacy before the
  incomprehensible is embodied in the monolith's blankness.
- **In Childhood's End, technology is a dead end.** The Overlords have
  mastered technology but cannot make the transcendental leap to the
  Overmind. Clarke's usual techno-optimism is *inverted*: the theme is
  that rational progress has limits, and what lies beyond is not more
  technology but transformation. The Overlords — the most advanced
  technological beings in the story — are *stuck*.
- **Clarke's two-stage myth of progress:** Critic John Huntington
  identified Clarke's recurring structure: first rational/technological
  progress, then transcendent evolution. "In his most far-reaching
  novels, technological progress fails to satisfy, and mankind advances
  not by inventing more competent machinery, but by mutating into a
  higher form of being."

Clarke himself said 2001 was "about concern with man's hierarchy in the
universe, which is pretty low" and "about the reactions of humanity to
the discovery of higher intelligence in the universe." Even this is
framed as *situation and reaction*, not as an abstract theme.

On advanced aliens: "Entities that are complex enough to be intelligent
are products of an evolutionary process. No matter how god-like they may
seem when we encounter them, they didn't start that way." The theme
resists simplification — these are not gods, but they might as well be.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** Clarke embeds theme through *what is
absent or withheld*. The monolith works because you never see who made
it. The Overlords work because they are powerful but limited. Theme
emerges from structural gaps, not from explanations.

### James P. Hogan — Inherit the Stars, Giants Series

Hogan's approach is distinct: he frames the "discovery of lost
civilization" theme as a **scientific mystery with no antagonist**.

Key structural choices:

- **No conflict as such.** *Inherit the Stars* has no villain. The
  tension is purely epistemological: scientists find a 50,000-year-old
  human skeleton on the Moon and must figure out how it got there. The
  theme of humanity's unknown origins is the *mystery itself*, not a
  message about it.
- **Theories are formulated, discarded, and replaced.** Hogan's
  philosophical commitment: theories should be based on empirical
  evidence, and if theory doesn't match facts, theory is discarded. This
  scientific method IS the narrative engine. The lost civilization
  reveals itself through iterative discovery, not revelation.
- **Origins from frustration with 2001.** Hogan wrote the novel because
  he was frustrated by 2001's confusing, effects-heavy ending. His bet
  with colleagues (five pounds he couldn't write a published novel)
  produced a book that replaced mystical transcendence with *methodical
  reconstruction*.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** A "lost civilization" theme does not
require a thematic label. It requires a *discovery process* — the
structure of how knowledge is assembled IS the theme. Hogan demonstrates
that epistemology (how we know what we know) can replace ontology (what
things mean) as the thematic anchor.

### Ursula K. Le Guin — The Dispossessed, The Left Hand of Darkness

Le Guin is the master of political/social themes that avoid didacticism.
Her technique: **the thought experiment as container**.

Key structural choices:

- **The subtitle does the work.** *The Dispossessed* is subtitled "An
  Ambiguous Utopia." Both societies (anarchist Anarres and capitalist
  Urras) are presented as flawed. Le Guin wrote Anarres as "a fragile,
  flawed utopia, but utopia nevertheless, utopia compared to oligarchic
  plutocracy." The ambiguity IS the theme — there is no correct answer.
- **Research replaces opinion.** Le Guin spent a year reading utopias and
  another year reading anarchist writers (Kropotkin, Paul Goodman) before
  writing. She "studied peace" rather than war. The depth of research
  allows the world to carry the argument; she doesn't need to editorialize.
- **Genre as camouflage.** When asked about presenting radical concepts
  without propaganda, Le Guin noted: "SF and fantasy slip under the wire
  a lot, you know? People just aren't looking for radical thought in a
  field the respectable critics define as escapist drivel."
- **Complexity over correctness.** On worldbuilding: "When I create
  another planet, another world, with a society on it, I try to hint at
  the complexity of the society I'm creating, instead of just referring
  to an empire or something like that." (Paris Review, Art of Fiction
  No. 221)
- **Social sciences as source material.** Le Guin drew on anthropology
  and sociology rather than physics: "The 'hard' science fiction writers
  dismiss everything except, well, physics, astronomy, and maybe
  chemistry. Biology, sociology, anthropology — that's not science to
  them, that's soft stuff. They're not that interested in what human
  beings do, really. But I am."
- **Fiction as discovery, not instruction.** On the purpose of fiction:
  "It's something that only human beings do... One of the things it does
  is lead you to recognize what you did not know before."

Le Guin was conscious of the tension: critic John Clute noted her later
writing sometimes suffered from "needing to speak responsibly about
important things." Didacticism crept in when she prioritized
responsibility over story.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** Build both sides of the argument into the
world as functioning societies. Let the reader compare. The subtitle
"An Ambiguous Utopia" is more honest than any theme statement: it tells
you the author knows there is no clean answer. Le Guin's themes work
because she did the reading — the world is specific enough to feel real,
and real enough to generate genuine disagreement.

### Frank Herbert — Dune

Herbert's thematic architecture is the densest of any author on this
list. His themes are not named — they are *fused into the ecology*.

Key structural choices:

- **The spice IS the theme.** Melange is simultaneously: an addictive
  substance, an economic resource controlling galactic politics, the
  source of prescient vision, a product of ecological cycles, and a
  substance that creates dependency in its users. It is not a metaphor
  for oil — it is a system that *operates like* resource dependency
  operates, at every level from personal to political.
- **The "Dune Genesis" essay (1980).** Herbert wrote: "I could begin to
  see the shape of a global problem, no part of it separated from any
  other — social ecology, political ecology, economic ecology." He
  conceived the world as a *single interconnected system*, not as
  separate themes.
- **The superhero warning.** Herbert's explicit theme: "Don't give over
  all of your critical faculties to people in power, no matter how
  admirable those people may appear to be. Beneath the hero's facade you
  will find a human being who makes human mistakes. Enormous problems
  arise when human mistakes are made on the grand scale available to a
  superhero." But note: this is stated in essays, not in the novels. The
  novels show Paul becoming the thing he fears.
- **Prescience as trap.** "Prescient insight operates as a peculiar trap
  for the prophet himself, where he can become the victim of what he
  knows." Paul's prescience doesn't free him — it imprisons him. The
  theme of knowledge-as-burden is dramatized through a character who can
  see the future and is *destroyed by seeing it*. Paul could not stop
  the jihad he foresaw. Leto II could, but only by becoming a worm.
- **The Golden Path.** Paul saw the path humanity needed to survive and
  flinched. Leto II saw it and committed, becoming a tyrant for 3,500
  years to teach humanity "a lesson they will remember in their bones" —
  that sheltered safety leads to extinction. The theme of freedom vs.
  security is not debated; it is enacted across millennia.
- **Ecology as origin.** Herbert visited Florence, Oregon in 1957, where
  the USDA was stabilizing sand dunes with European beachgrass. He read
  over 200 books of ecological research. The worldbuilding is grounded
  in *actual science*, and the theme of ecological interdependence grows
  from that foundation.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** Herbert's themes are inseparable from the
world's physical systems. You cannot explain Dune's ecology without
explaining its politics, economics, and religion. This is the gold
standard: a world where removing any one thematic thread causes the
others to collapse.

### Iain M. Banks — Culture Series

Banks frames the tension between advanced and less-advanced civilizations
through a **utopia that gets its hands dirty**.

Key structural choices:

- **The Culture is explicitly political.** Banks was direct: "The Culture
  is hippy commies with hyper-weapons and a deep distrust of both
  Marketolatry and Greedism." In a 1994 Usenet post: "Succinctly;
  socialism within, anarchy without." He did not hide his politics — but
  the novels dramatize the *consequences* of those politics, not the
  politics themselves.
- **The Minds embody the intelligence-gap theme.** Banks used the term
  "sentient machine" instead of "artificial intelligence" to highlight
  emotionality and embodiment. The Culture has three tiers of machine
  sentience: task-specific robots, human-level "drones" with full
  citizenship, and super-human "Minds" that run ships and orbitals. The
  theme of what happens when intelligence outstrips comprehension is
  *built into the social structure*.
- **Dirty tricks as thematic engine.** The Culture engages in blackmail,
  mercenary employment, regime change, and false flag operations through
  its Special Circumstances division. "Even the Culture throws away its
  usual moral rule-book" to prevent atrocities. This is the central
  tension: can a utopia remain utopian while intervening in non-utopian
  societies? The answer is complicated, and Banks lets it stay
  complicated.
- **Failure is structural.** In *Look to Windward*, the Chelgrian
  civil war is the Culture getting it wrong. Banks said the Culture
  admits its mistakes, and "that lesson goes into the statistics and
  changes them, making subsequent interventions less risk-keen and more
  likely to work better." The theme of intervention is not argued — it
  is *iterated*.
- **The Culture was a pragmatic invention.** Banks created it as "a
  single-use solution to a particular problem" — a society that would be
  on the side of the angels but willing to use people like Zakalwe to do
  its justified dirty work. The setting grew from a narrative need, not
  from a theme statement.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** Banks demonstrates that you can be
explicit about a setting's politics without being didactic, as long as the
*consequences* of those politics drive the plot. The Culture's moral
failures are more interesting than its moral victories. Build the utopia,
then test it.

### Steven Erikson — Malazan Book of the Fallen

Erikson's approach to compassion as a theme is the most instructive for
avoiding GenAI failure modes, because the theme is both central and
never simplified.

Key structural choices:

- **Three million words of plea.** Erikson stated explicitly: "There is a
  general theme throughout the ten-book series: compassion and the
  avenues that people can take to achieve that compassion through their
  efforts and the choices they make in their lives." He used multiple
  points of view because he "wanted to approach compassion from as many
  angles as I possibly could," making it "a three million word plea for
  compassion."
- **Compassion is enacted, not discussed.** The iconic Itkovian scene in
  *Memories of Ice*: a Shield Anvil who takes on the grief and suffering
  of others, literally absorbing their pain. His refrain — "I am not yet
  done" — as he absorbs more suffering than should be survivable. The
  theme is embodied in a character *doing something*, not stating
  something.
- **Brutality is the necessary foundation.** Erikson: "None of the
  resonance would occur without an adherence to authenticity, which
  comes from the use of details touching on every sense in a way that
  immerses the reader, and from characters who feel real living in a
  solid physical world with recognizable internal landscapes." The
  world's cruelty is what makes compassion meaningful. Without the
  brutality, the compassion would be cheap.
- **Writing through difficulty.** "When a scene becomes brutally
  difficult, don't escape it. Push through. That pressure is where you
  learn what it means to be a writer." Erikson's craft advice mirrors his
  thematic approach: don't flinch from the hard parts.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** Compassion works as a theme in Malazan
because the world earns it. The suffering is specific and authentic; the
compassion is specific and costly. A theme of compassion in a gentle
world is meaningless. A theme of compassion in a world where compassion
gets you killed — that resonates.

### Brandon Sanderson — Cosmere

Sanderson's distinctive contribution: **magic systems as thematic
expression**.

Key structural choices:

- **Allomancy encodes class.** In Mistborn, allomancy (metal-based magic)
  was given by the Lord Ruler to his early supporters — the nobility.
  Interbreeding between nobles and commoners (skaa) was made illegal
  specifically to prevent the skaa from gaining allomantic powers. The
  magic system IS the class system. The revolution's arc is inseparable
  from who can and cannot burn metals.
- **Sanderson's Last Law:** "A good magic system should be interconnected
  with the world around it." Magic does not take place in a vacuum — it
  relates to ecology, religion, economics, warfare, and politics. This
  is a design principle that ensures magic carries thematic weight.
- **Oaths as character arcs.** In The Stormlight Archive, Knights Radiant
  progress by swearing increasingly demanding Ideals — oaths that
  represent genuine personal growth. The Ideals are different for each
  order, and each order's theme shapes the kind of growth required.
  Elsecallers pursue progress and self-improvement. The magical
  progression is character development made literal.
- **Broken people bond with spren.** The Stormlight Archive connects
  mental health to magical capacity: characters dealing with depression,
  dissociative identity, addiction, and PTSD are the ones who can bond
  with spren and become Knights Radiant. The magic system's thematic
  argument: brokenness is not weakness; it is the prerequisite for
  growth.
- **The periodic-table aesthetic.** Sanderson wanted allomancy to feel
  like "an industrial-age science, a good hybrid of science and magic,"
  inspired by alchemy and "a periodic table that actually works." The
  aesthetic register of the magic — systematic, categorizable,
  discoverable — is itself a thematic statement about the world's
  relationship with knowledge.

**Takeaway for worldbuilding:** If your world has a magic system (or a
technology system, or a resonance system), make it *do thematic work*.
Sanderson's systems are not window dressing — they encode the world's
social structure, its values, and its understanding of what makes people
powerful. The rules of the system ARE the thematic argument.

## 2. What Separates Great from Good in Thematic Handling

### Great themes are systems, not statements

Every author above embodies their themes in world-mechanics:

| Author | Theme | Mechanical Expression |
|--------|-------|-----------------------|
| Asimov | Individual vs. collective | Psychohistory (works on masses, fails on individuals) |
| Clarke | Humanity before the incomprehensible | The monolith (present, silent, transformative) |
| Hogan | How we know what we know | Scientific investigation as plot engine |
| Le Guin | No perfect society | Two flawed worlds in structural comparison |
| Herbert | Ecology is politics is religion is power | Spice (one substance, every system) |
| Banks | Can utopia get its hands dirty? | Special Circumstances (the Culture's shadow) |
| Erikson | Compassion in a brutal world | Characters who choose mercy at personal cost |
| Sanderson | Systems encode social structure | Magic tied to class, oaths, mental health |

**The pattern:** None of these themes can be separated from the world they
inhabit. You cannot state the theme of Dune without explaining spice,
worms, the Fremen, CHOAM, the Bene Gesserit, and the Spacing Guild.
The theme IS the system.

### Great themes generate genuine disagreement

A theme that nobody would argue against is not a theme — it is a platitude.

- **Dune:** "Should you follow a charismatic leader?" is a real question.
  Paul is genuinely compelling. Readers *want* to follow him, which is
  Herbert's entire point.
- **The Dispossessed:** "Is anarchism viable?" Le Guin subtitled it
  "An Ambiguous Utopia" because she knew the answer was not clean.
  Anarres has real problems. Urras has real virtues.
- **The Culture:** "Should an advanced civilization intervene in less
  advanced ones?" Banks lets the Culture get it wrong. The Chelgrian
  catastrophe is genuine failure, not a straw man.
- **Malazan:** "Is compassion worth its cost?" Itkovian dies. Coltaine
  dies. The compassionate characters pay in blood. The world does not
  reward mercy.

**Test for a theme:** Can you write a compelling character who argues the
opposite position? If not, the theme is too easy.

### Great themes are messy and contradictory

Herbert's Dune contains multiple themes that pull against each other:
the danger of charismatic leaders, the ecological necessity of
adaptation, the trap of prescience, the dependency of empires on
critical resources. These themes do not resolve neatly. Paul is both
savior and destroyer. The Fremen are both liberated and weaponized.

Le Guin's Anarres is both a genuine utopia and a society with its own
forms of coercion (social pressure replacing law). Banks's Culture is
both morally superior and morally compromised.

**The principle:** If your themes resolve cleanly, they are not themes —
they are morals. Themes are tensions. Morals are conclusions.

## 3. Common GenAI Failure Modes for Themes

### The "[Abstract Noun] of [Abstract Noun]" pattern

GenAI gravitates toward: "The Cost of Knowledge," "The Burden of Power,"
"The Price of Freedom," "The Weight of Legacy." These are not themes —
they are *theme-shaped nouns*. They sound thematic without encoding any
actual tension.

**Why it fails:** A real theme generates disagreement. "The Cost of
Knowledge" presupposes that knowledge has a cost — nobody argues the
other side. A real thematic tension would be: "Knowledge that imprisons
vs. ignorance that liberates" — but even this is too neat. Herbert's
version: Paul *sees the future* and it *destroys him*, but refusing to
see would have destroyed everyone else. The cost is not abstract; it is
specific, personal, and tied to the world's mechanics.

### Themes that are stated rather than demonstrated

LLMs produce text where characters *discuss* themes rather than *live*
them. Research confirms: "LLM-generated text is often hackneyed and rife
with clichés, lacks nuance, subtext, and rhetorical complexity."

**The diagnostic:** If you can remove the thematic dialogue and the plot
still works, the theme is decorative, not structural. In Foundation,
you cannot remove psychohistory — it IS the plot. In Dune, you cannot
remove spice — it IS the economy, the religion, and the power structure.

### Themes without genuine tension

GenAI-generated themes often have no one arguing the other side:
- "Cooperation is better than conflict" — who disagrees?
- "Power corrupts" — where is the compelling counter-example?
- "Nature must be preserved" — what is the genuine cost?

Compare Herbert: Paul *must* become the thing he hates, because the
alternative is worse. The theme has teeth because both sides are real.

### Themes that are too neat

Research on AI fiction: "Only two out of nine LLMs exhibited consistent
worldview, while the rest are self-conflicting, and subsequent analysis
of stories generated by four models revealed a strikingly uniform
narrative pattern."

Real themes are internally contradictory. Le Guin's anarchist utopia
has its own authoritarianism. Banks's socialist paradise employs
assassins. Erikson's compassion theme is set in a world where
compassion is lethal. The messiness is not a bug — it is what makes
themes feel true.

### Generic vocabulary and abstraction

AI-generated text overuses "tapestry," "realm," "beacon," "cacophony,"
"delve," "underscore," "harness," "illuminate," and "facilitate." It
replaces specific facts with generic positive descriptions. It uses
evocative but empty nouns.

**For worldbuilding specifically:** AI will produce "the ancient
resonance that echoes through the shattered ring" when what you need is
"the Sithari water-clock guild charges two glass tokens per hour for
resonance tuning, and they've been in a pricing dispute with Fermata
Station since the last Accord review."

## 4. How Great Series Encode Themes Structurally

### Foundation: Psychohistory IS the theme

The system has rules: works on masses, fails on individuals, requires a
large population, can be disrupted by mutants. Every rule is a thematic
statement:

- Works on masses → collective patterns matter more than individual will
- Fails on individuals → but individuals can break the pattern
- Requires large population → the math of history only works at scale
- Can be disrupted → determinism is conditional, not absolute

The Seldon Crises are not plot points that illustrate a theme — they are
the theme in operation. Each crisis tests whether the pattern holds.
When it doesn't (the Mule), the *failure* is the most important thematic
moment.

### Dune: Spice IS the theme

Melange connects every system:

- **Ecology:** Produced by sandworms, which require a specific biome
- **Economy:** Controls interstellar travel (Spacing Guild navigators)
- **Politics:** Whoever controls Arrakis controls the spice
- **Religion:** Enables prescient vision (Bene Gesserit, Paul)
- **Personal:** Addictive; withdrawal is fatal
- **Temporal:** Enables foresight, which traps the seer

Remove spice and the entire world collapses — not because it is a
convenient plot device, but because Herbert designed the world as a
single integrated system where every component depends on every other.

### Culture: The Minds ARE the theme

The Minds are superintelligent, emotionally complex, and functionally
run everything. The humans in the Culture live in a post-scarcity
paradise that they did not build and cannot maintain without the Minds.

This IS the theme of intelligence outstripping comprehension:
- Humans in the Culture are free because Minds handle everything
- But this freedom is contingent on entities they cannot fully understand
- The Minds make moral decisions on humanity's behalf
- Special Circumstances operates in shadows humans don't see

The Culture is a utopia that depends on benevolent superintelligence.
The theme is not "AI good" or "AI bad" — it is "what does it mean to
live in a society run by entities smarter than you?" Banks leaves this
genuinely open.

### Malazan: Compassion IS the counterweight

Erikson's world is genuinely brutal — wars of extermination, gods who
treat mortals as pawns, grinding poverty, institutional cruelty. The
compassion theme works because:

- It is chosen, not mandated. Characters like Itkovian and Whiskeyjack
  choose compassion at personal cost.
- It is not rewarded. Compassionate characters die. The world does not
  validate their choices.
- It is not universal. Many characters choose cruelty, pragmatism, or
  indifference, and these choices are presented as understandable.
- It accumulates. Across three million words, the cumulative effect of
  characters choosing mercy creates the thematic argument that no single
  scene could make.

The theme is not "compassion is good." The theme is "compassion is
what humans keep choosing even when it costs them everything, and that
choice is what makes them worth writing about."

## 5. How to Name Themes for a Worldbuilding Project

### The professional framework: theme topic vs. theme statement

Writing craft distinguishes between:

- **Theme topic:** One or two words. The subject being explored. Example:
  "freedom," "knowledge," "dependency."
- **Theme statement:** A complete sentence. What the work argues about
  that subject. Example: "Freedom requires constant vigilance against
  its own institutions."

The theme topic is useful for organizing subplots and ensuring every
character interacts with the central concern. The theme statement is
useful for the main character's arc. Both are internal tools — neither
appears in the text.

### The game design framework: design pillars

Game development uses "design pillars" — 3-8 fundamental parameters
that orient the entire team. These are functional, not evocative:

- *Undertale:* The rhythm of dialogue and music matters more than
  the tale itself (narrative as core pillar)
- *The Last of Us:* Partner relationship (emotional connection as pillar)
- *Breath of the Wild:* Exploration and travel (spatial freedom as pillar)

Pillars are short, concrete, and testable. "Does this feature serve the
pillar?" is a design question with a yes/no answer.

### The TV series bible framework

Ron Moore's *Battlestar Galactica* bible (2003) demonstrates the
professional approach:

- Themes are stated in terms of what the show *does*, not what it
  *means*: "nothing less than the reinvention of the science fiction
  television series"
- Three-tiered story structure: series arc (top), season arc (middle),
  episode arc (bottom)
- Themes are enforced through structural rules: "naturalistic science
  fiction" — no bumpy-headed aliens, no technobabble, no empty heroics

The bible does not contain a "themes" section that lists abstract nouns.
It contains a *tone section* that describes what the show feels like and
a *rules section* that describes what the show will and will not do.

### Recommendations for The Glass Frontier

Based on this research:

**Do not name themes as "[Abstract Noun] of [Abstract Noun]."** This is
the single most reliable marker of GenAI-generated worldbuilding.

**Instead, use one of these registers:**

1. **Structural/mechanical:** "Resonance creates dependency — whoever
   controls the source controls the settlements that rely on it." This
   is a system description that carries thematic weight. It sounds like
   worldbuilding, not like a theme statement.

2. **Question-form:** "What happens when the technology that keeps
   everyone alive was built by people who are gone?" This frames the
   theme as an unresolved question, which is honest — the world doesn't
   know the answer either.

3. **Design-pillar form:** Short, testable, functional. "Frontier
   communities depend on systems they don't understand." Every lore entry
   can be tested against this: does this entry show or complicate that
   dependency?

4. **Character-action form:** "People keep building things even when
   the last builders' work shattered." This frames the theme through
   what characters DO, not what the world MEANS.

**Naming themes internally (for project tracking) vs. externally (in
lore entries):**

- **Internally:** Use functional descriptions. "The resonance-dependency
  tension" is fine for a work-tracking document. It tells collaborators
  what to think about when writing entries that touch on resonance.
- **In lore entries:** Never name the theme. The lore is an encyclopedia
  written by someone who lives in the world. They don't know they're
  in a themed world. They describe systems, factions, trade routes,
  and conflicts. The theme emerges from the pattern.

**The ultimate test from this research:** Can you remove the thematic
language from your project documents and still see the theme operating
in the world's systems? If yes, the theme is structural. If no, the
theme is decorative.
